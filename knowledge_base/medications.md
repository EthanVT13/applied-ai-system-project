# Pet Medication Best Practices

## Timing and Consistency
Administering medications at the same time each day maintains steady drug levels in the bloodstream and reduces the chance of missed doses. Set a phone reminder or tie the medication to a fixed daily event (morning feeding, bedtime) to build a reliable habit.

## Missed Doses
If a dose is missed and it is not close to the next scheduled time, give it as soon as you remember. If it is almost time for the next dose, skip the missed dose and continue the regular schedule. Never double up doses unless specifically instructed by a veterinarian.

## Oral Medications
Hiding pills in a small amount of soft food (peanut butter, cream cheese, or commercial pill pockets) is usually the easiest method. Give the medicated treat by itself before the regular meal so hunger motivates acceptance. If your pet becomes pill-savvy, crush the tablet (only if safe to do so — ask your vet) and mix it into food.

Liquid medications can be drawn into a syringe and squirted gently into the side of the mouth between the cheek and teeth, allowing the pet to swallow naturally.

## Topical Treatments
Apply topical spot-on treatments (flea/tick preventatives, ear medications) at the base of the skull or between the shoulder blades where the pet cannot lick them. Part the fur to reach the skin. Allow to dry completely before touching the area or letting children handle the pet.

## Eye and Ear Drops
Approach from behind or beside the pet, not head-on, to minimize stress. Gently tilt the head so the affected eye or ear faces upward. Administer the prescribed number of drops, then hold the head gently for a few seconds. Reward immediately with a favorite treat. Clean any discharge from the area with a separate damp cotton ball before applying drops.

## Heartworm Prevention
Heartworm disease is transmitted by mosquitoes and is potentially fatal. Monthly preventatives must be given on a strict schedule year-round (or as advised by your vet based on local risk). Missing even one monthly dose can leave a pet unprotected. Record the date of each dose and set a recurring calendar reminder.

## Flea and Tick Prevention
Year-round prevention is recommended in most regions. Apply on the same calendar day each month. Check your pet for ticks after every outdoor activity, even if using preventatives.

## Prescription Medications — Safety Notes
Never share prescription medications between pets without veterinary approval. Drug doses are calculated by weight and species; what is safe for one animal can be dangerous for another. Store all medications in a cool, dry place out of reach of children and pets. Dispose of unused medications safely — do not flush down the drain.

## Signs of Adverse Reactions
Watch for vomiting, diarrhea, lethargy, facial swelling, hives, or difficulty breathing within 30 minutes to a few hours of giving any new medication. Contact your veterinarian or an emergency animal clinic immediately if any of these occur.
